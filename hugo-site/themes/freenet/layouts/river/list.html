How it works

Wait, no servers?

Most chat apps send your messages to servers a company runs. River sends them to Freenet, a network made of its users' own computers. Each room lives on the network itself, so it keeps working even if the person who made it goes offline, and nobody has a server to shut down, snoop on, or bill.

Chat that no one owns

The things that make River different are the things it leaves out.

No servers

Every room lives on Freenet, hosted by the network's own peers. There's no backend for anyone to run, pay for, or take down.

No account

No sign-up, no phone number, no email. Your identity is a key on your device, not a row in someone's database.

Encrypted rooms

Private rooms are end-to-end encrypted, and every message is signed, so any peer can host a room without being able to forge or read it.

Scales with demand

The more people a room has, the more peers cache it, so it gets faster and sturdier as it grows, not slower. The opposite of the server model, where traffic is a bill.

Getting in

You install Freenet, not River

River isn't a separate app to download. You install Freenet once, and any number of apps, River included, run on top of it and open right in your browser.

  1. 1

    Install Freenet

    A small background app runs a Freenet peer on your machine. River and every other Freenet app run on top of it.

  2. 2

    Click an invite

    Get an invite to the official room, or from a friend. Rooms are invite-only, so you always know who let someone in.

  3. 3

    You're in the room

    River opens in your browser. Create your own rooms and share their invite links the same way.

Why not Matrix, Discord, or Signal?

Everyone else runs a server, or keeps you online

Other systems decentralize to different degrees, but each one still puts something between you and the room.

Discord, SignalCentral servers, operated by a company.
MatrixFederated homeservers, each one still run by someone.
NostrRelays, which are servers.
Briar, ToxServerless, but device-to-device: both people must be online, and there's no shared room the network keeps for you.
RiverEach room lives on Freenet itself. No company server, no homeserver to pick, and rooms that persist when their creator is offline.

Privacy by design

No middleman to leak

Privacy in River isn't a mode you switch on. It's what's left when you remove the company, the account, and the server.

{{ partial "inline/rv-check.html" . }}
No central server to subpoena or breach.Rooms live on the network; there's no company database to hand over or hack.
{{ partial "inline/rv-check.html" . }}
No phone number tied to your identity.You never hand over a number or an email to sign up.
{{ partial "inline/rv-check.html" . }}
End-to-end encryption on private rooms.Only members can read them, not the peers hosting the data.
{{ partial "inline/rv-check.html" . }}
Open source and auditable.No blockchain, no token, no coins. Just code you can read on GitHub.

Private rooms hide content, not shape: peers can still see that a room exists and roughly how active it is. If your threat model needs to hide that too, River alone isn't enough yet.
