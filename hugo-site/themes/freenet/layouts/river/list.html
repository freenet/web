How it works

Wait, no servers?

Most chat apps send your messages to servers a company runs. River sends them to Freenet, a network made of its users' own computers. Each room lives on the network itself, so it keeps working even if the person who made it goes offline, and nobody has a server to shut down, snoop on, or bill.

Chat that no one owns

The things that make River different are the things it leaves out.

No servers

Every room lives on Freenet, hosted by the network's own peers. There's no backend for anyone to run, pay for, or take down.

No account

No sign-up, no phone number, no email. Your identity is a key on your device, not a row in someone's database.

Encrypted rooms

Private rooms are end-to-end encrypted, and every message is signed, so any peer can host a room without being able to forge or read it.

Scales with demand

The more people a room has, the more peers cache it, so it gets faster and sturdier as it grows, not slower. The opposite of the server model, where traffic is a bill.

Getting in

You install Freenet, not River

River isn't a separate app to download. You install Freenet once, and any number of apps, River included, run on top of it and open right in your browser.

  1. 1

    Install Freenet

    A small background app runs a Freenet peer on your machine. River and every other Freenet app run on top of it.

  2. 2

    Click an invite

    Grab a free invite to the official room, no friend needed, or use one a friend sent you. Rooms are invite-only, so you always know who let someone in.

  3. 3

    You're in the room

    River opens in your browser. Create your own rooms and share their invite links the same way.

Why not Signal, WhatsApp, or Matrix?

Everyone else runs on servers someone controls

Any single operator can be pressured, subpoenaed, breached, or decide to cut you off. It doesn't take bad intent, just one point everyone has to trust. River has none.

WhatsAppEncrypted, but it runs on Meta's servers and identifies you by your phone number.
SignalExcellent encryption, run by a nonprofit. But it still runs on central servers, and needs a phone number to sign up.
Discord, SlackA company's servers, which it can read.

Straight talk

Where River is today

River and Freenet are alpha. Here's what works now and what to expect, so nothing surprises you after you install.

Works today

  • Join the live official room
  • Create your own rooms
  • Public rooms, and end-to-end encrypted private rooms
  • One-click invite links
  • Command-line client (riverctl)

Still alpha

  • Desktop only (Windows, macOS, Linux); no mobile app yet
  • Your Freenet peer sends diagnostic telemetry (peer activity, general system info) to help debug the network
  • Metadata isn't hidden: whether a room exists, its size, and message timing are observable
  • Not for sensitive communications yet
  • The network changes fast, and peers auto-update

Built by a nonprofit, not an ad company

River and Freenet are built by Freenet Project Inc, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. No ads, no investors, no business that runs on your data. Just open-source software that runs on a network its users own.
